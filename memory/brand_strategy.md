## 8. How Technology Should Be Positioned

### The rule
Technology is a **means of service delivery**, not the service itself.

### What this means in practice
- Never lead with "AI" or "smart" features in primary copy
- Technology enables CA. Prasad to serve more clients effectively — don't hide this, but don't make it the headline
- The client's experience of technology should be: easy communication, faster turnaround, no paperwork confusion
- Future AI Tax Assistant should be positioned as a *supplementary tool* with CA oversight — never as a replacement for professional advice

### Approved framing
✓ "Technology-enabled for faster turnaround"
✓ "Digital-first communication — phone, WhatsApp, or email"
✓ "Secure document exchange"
✓ "Automated reminders so you never miss a deadline"

### Disapproved framing
✗ "AI-powered tax filing"
✗ "Smart tax assistant"
✗ "Automated compliance"
✗ "Technology transforms your tax experience"

## 11. Tone Guidelines

### Always
- Use plain English. If a technical term is necessary (GSTR-3B, DTAA), define it briefly.
- Be specific. "2–3 working days" beats "quickly". "ICAI member" beats "certified expert".
- Use the second person ("you", "your") in client-facing copy.
- Use the founder's full name ("CA. Prasad" in informal contexts, "CA. V.V.N.Prasad. Gupta" in formal contexts).
- End copy blocks with the outcome, not the process.

### Never
- Use exclamation marks in body copy or section headings.
- Use: "empower", "journey", "seamless", "effortless", "unlock", "game-changing", "cutting-edge", "powerful AI"
- Overpromise on AI capabilities (current or future)
- Describe the service as "easy" — it implies the client's problem is trivial. Instead: "we make it manageable"
- Use passive voice for key claims. Say "CA. Prasad reviews every return" not "every return is reviewed"
